文俶 Wen Shu: The Artistic Life of Women's Boudoir Painter

Wen Shu, a female painter of the late Ming Dynasty, courtesy name Duanrong, was the great-granddaughter of the renowned artist Wen Zhengming and grew up in a scholarly family in Jiangnan. Born into seclusion, she used delicate brushwork to depict flowers, insects, and plants, becoming one of the earliest recorded talented female painters in Chinese art history. In an era when women were largely excluded from public artistic life, her boudoir became her main stage for creation.

Drawing inspiration from the intimate world of her home, Wen Shu spent years studying nature, culminating in Illustrations of Minerals, Insects, Plants, and Trees, a project of 1,316 illustrations covering 1,070 herbs. Though her subjects were limited, she used flowers, insects, and plants as her language to express independent thought, subtle emotions, and the rhythms of her personal world.

Her paintings were once collected by emperors of the Qing dynasty.
